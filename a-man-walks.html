A man walks


A man sinks. He sinks into the darkness, into nothingness, forgotten. You don’t see him.

“There’s a man walking, mom!”

But you don’t see him; the man walks, you don’t see his feet, you don’t see his arched back covered by the coat, you don’t see his crooked cane, he walks.

“Mom, where is he going?”

Even pointing wouldn’t help because you simply can’t see him, it’s not to blame on your dimmed eyes or the sticky fog weighing on our bones, you just couldn’t see him anyway.

“No mom, it’s not a game. He’s walking, look!”

And you don’t see his slow and steady steps, you don’t see his heavy, stiff shoes, irritating on the ankles from wear, his elegant hat crushed by time, his dark, worn-out trousers, you don’t see his tired hands, the exposed nape, the rough, dry, tough skin. You can’t see him because he is halfway sunk and isn’t going anywhere. He stumbles and sinks his soles into the mire, relentlessly continuing his immobile, unsteady yet perpetual walk. As he approaches, more details of his motionless stride become noticeable.

“But why isn’t he moving, mom?”

You say nothing, you don’t see, and what you say is just air to my ears, you don’t see that man. You don’t see the coarse mustache still carrying the scent of pipe and gin, the gray and white hairs in his nose; you don’t see the tears he never had the courage to cry, you don’t see the bitter fatigue, blind to his limit that keeps him tethered to an empty destiny.

You see my fear, pure, my terrified eyes, my now-lost eyes, a swollen tear on the verge of the abyss falls alone, you see my pierced soul. You worry, sowing useless panic; you try to save me, but you don’t try to understand.

You can’t see. Frustration. Anguish.

“Mom, why? Why does he keep walking if he doesn’t move?”

You go mad, you despair, a scene that drags on for long, but I don’t hear your screams, your crying, your desperate caresses. I see but don’t hear, you hear but don’t see.

The man runs, he flees, while remaining in his swamp.

I see him, or rather, I cannot help but see him: every time I avert my gaze, I find him right there waiting for me, in front of me, right in front of my nose, as if he were a puppet whose strings are fixed to the tips of my fingers, beyond a milky and insistent stage of fog. I wish to reach him. I start running, I don’t hear your desperate cries, I don’t feel your nails tearing at my clothes in vain trying to stop me, and I don’t hear your prayers, your pleas. You’ve scratched my arms and back, you can’t hold me back, and all I have left of you is a tug. I run to the man, but he is unreachable, and I run forever sinking into his emptiness.

You don’t see me anymore. The fog. The escape. And you wouldn’t see me anyway.
